General Information

The Museum of Islamic Art is the largest museum of Islamic antiquities in the world, displaying diverse collections of Islamic art from Egypt, India, China, Andalusia, the Levant, the Arabian Peninsula, North Africa, and other parts of the Islamic world. The idea for its establishment began during the reign of Khedive Ismail in 1869, and it was inaugurated by Khedive Abbas Hilmi II in 1903. The museum closed for renovations in 2003 and reopened in 2010.
In 2014, the museum building and approximately 179 artifacts were damaged in a bombing of the Cairo Security Directorate building opposite it.
The museum was subsequently closed for restoration and renovation, and it was reopened in 2017 by order of the President of the Republic. The museum houses the largest collection of Islamic art artifacts from across the Islamic world, covering a wide range of subjects including astronomy, medicine, architecture, and more.
The materials used in their creation are also diverse, ranging from woodwork and decorated ceramics to manuscripts. Among the museum's most prized treasures is a key to the Kaaba dating back to the Mamluk era.
